## Overview
This skill provides a method for conducting break-even analysis, which identifies the point at which total costs and total revenue are equal, meaning there is no net loss or gain. It's crucial for understanding business viability, pricing strategies, and investment decisions.

## Usage
Provide details on fixed costs, variable costs per unit, and the selling price per unit. The skill will outline the calculation of the break-even point and timeframe.

## Steps
1.  **Identify Fixed Costs**: Sum all costs that do not change with the volume of goods or services produced (e.g., rent, salaries, insurance).
2.  **Identify Variable Costs Per Unit**: Determine costs that vary directly with the production of one additional unit (e.g., raw materials, direct labor).
3.  **Determine Selling Price Per Unit**: Define the revenue generated from selling one unit of product or service.
4.  **Calculate Contribution Margin Per Unit**: Subtract variable cost per unit from the selling price per unit.
5.  **Calculate Break-Even Point in Units**: Divide total fixed costs by the contribution margin per unit.
6.  **Calculate Break-Even Point in Revenue**: Divide total fixed costs by the contribution margin ratio (contribution margin per unit / selling price per unit).
7.  **Estimate Break-Even Timeframe**: Based on projected sales volume and the break-even point in units, determine how long it will take to reach break-even.

## Example (Conceptual)
```
Input: "Fixed Costs: $10,000. Variable Cost per Unit: $5. Selling Price per Unit: $15. Expected Sales: 1,000 units/month."

Output (Conceptual):
-   **Contribution Margin per Unit**: $15 - $5 = $10
-   **Break-Even Point in Units**: $10,000 / $10 = 1,000 units
-   **Break-Even Point in Revenue**: 1,000 units * $15 = $15,000
-   **Break-Even Timeframe**: Given 1,000 units sold per month, the break-even point is reached in 1 month.
-   **Assumptions**: Costs and selling price remain constant, sales volume is consistent.
```

## Pitfalls
*   **Inaccurate Cost Data**: Misclassifying fixed vs. variable costs or incorrect cost figures.
*   **Ignoring Multiple Products**: Break-even analysis becomes more complex with multiple products and different contribution margins.
*   **Static Analysis**: Not accounting for changes in costs, prices, or sales volume over time.
*   **Focusing Only on Financials**: Ignoring market demand, competition, or operational capacity.
*   **Short-Term Focus**: While good for short-term planning, it doesn't account for long-term growth or strategic investments.
